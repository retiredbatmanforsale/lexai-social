$500 billion added to SpaceX's valuation right before its IPO, on the strength of a single contract.

A contract that can be cancelled with 90 days' notice.

--

On May 20, 2026, SpaceX filed to go public at a $1.75–2 trillion valuation — a $40–80B raise that would possibly surpass Saudi Aramco's all-time IPO record. But that valuation has a story underneath it:

📈 SpaceX was already real — ~$18.7B revenue in 2025, mostly Starlink, projected toward $22–24B
🛰️ Then Anthropic signed on for ~$15B/year in compute ($1.25B/month through 2029) — almost as much as SpaceX's entire 2025 revenue, from a single customer
⚓ Analysts now treat that one deal as the valuation anchor — and it may have pulled SpaceX from ~$1.25T to $1.75T+

So ~$500B of market cap is resting on one counterparty. Now look at the counterparty:

→ Anthropic is paying ~$15B/year to rent the Memphis "Colossus" cluster from xAI — which SpaceX absorbed in February. xAI builds Grok, a direct Claude competitor. So Anthropic is renting compute from its rival, inside the very company about to go public. When nothing else exists at that power scale, that's not a market rate. It's a hostage premium.

→ The kicker: xAI had already moved its own training to a newer cluster (Colossus 2), leaving Colossus 1 at ~11% utilization. Anthropic is effectively paying $15B/year to fill a competitor's older, half-empty data center — and running its most sensitive training on hardware a direct competitor owns and operates. Either contractual firewalls make that a non-issue, or it's the single biggest competitive-intelligence exposure in AI right now. The S-1 doesn't say which.

→ And Anthropic is pre-IPO, burning capital fast, while also carrying a 10-year, $100B AWS deal and a multiyear Google TPU deal in parallel. That's not diversification — it's proof no single vendor can feed it. If its fundraising stumbles, that $15B/year commitment weakens, and SpaceX's stock reprices around a risk that was never legible in the S-1.

--

A rocket company didn't diversify into AI. It accidentally became a critical node in the compute supply chain — and is now pricing its public debut on a competitor's customer staying alive.

I genuinely don't get the games these people play. Billionaires, trillionaires, quadzillionaires.

For perspective: $500B is more than Reliance, HDFC Bank and TCS — India's three most valuable companies — combined. In 2026, not a single Indian company is left in the world's top 100. One cancellable contract is worth more than all of that.

Real valuation, or related-party engineering? What's your read?
